# Physical Therapy Timing, Utilization, and Outcomes  
## Annotated Bibliography (with Hill’s Criteria Analysis)

This repository supports a broader analysis of prior authorization and access to physical therapy by examining the strength of the evidence base, particularly through the lens of Hill’s criteria for causal inference.

### Note on Plausibility and Coherence

In this bibliography, plausibility and coherence are currently rated as “strong but possibly tautological.” This reflects a specific limitation in how these criteria function in this body of literature.

Many of the proposed mechanisms linking earlier physical therapy to improved outcomes (such as reduced downstream utilization, avoidance of escalation, or substitution away from less desirable interventions) are conceptually reasonable and internally consistent. In that sense, they satisfy plausibility and coherence.

However, these same mechanisms are often closely aligned with the outcomes being measured. As a result, the explanation and the observation can become difficult to disentangle: findings that earlier physical therapy is associated with reduced utilization are supported by mechanisms that largely restate that relationship in causal terms. This creates a risk of circular reasoning, where the observed association reinforces the plausibility of the mechanism without independently strengthening causal inference.

For this reason, plausibility and coherence are treated here as supportive but limited criteria. They are necessary for causal interpretation, but not sufficient, particularly in the absence of stronger evidence from temporality, experiment, or robust control of confounding.

## Fritz et al. (2012)

Fritz, J. M., Childs, J. D., Wainner, R. S., & Flynn, T. W.  
**Primary care referral of patients with low back pain to physical therapy: impact on future health care utilization and costs.**  
*Spine*, 37(25), 2114–2121.  
https://doi.org/10.1097/BRS.0b013e31825d32f5  

This is a retrospective claims-based study of patients with a new primary care consultation for low back pain. It found that early physical therapy was associated with lower subsequent low-back-pain-related costs and lower downstream utilization than delayed physical therapy. Under Hill’s criteria, the paper supports partially supports temporality because timing of PT precedes later utilization and cost outcomes, and it shows moderate strength of association for utilization and cost signals. It also supports plausibility and coherence, since earlier conservative management could reasonably reduce later imaging, injections, and other escalation. Its main weakness is causal identification: because the design is observational, experiment is absent, specificity is weak, consistency depends on related observational studies rather than direct replication, and biological gradient is not clearly established. This is good evidence for association and policy relevance around utilization, weaker evidence for causal claims about patient outcomes.

### Summary  
Retrospective claims-based study showing early physical therapy is associated with lower downstream healthcare utilization and costs.

### Hill’s Criteria Assessment  
- **Temporality**: Weekly supported (retrospective)
- **Strength**: Moderate (utilization, not outcomes)  
- **Consistency**: Moderate across similar observational studies  
- **Plausibility**: Strong but possibly tautological 
- **Coherence**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Experiment**: Not satisfied  
- **Specificity**: Weak  
- **Biological Gradient**: Not demonstrated  

### Interpretation  
Evidence for association with utilization patterns; limited causal inference for clinical outcomes.

## Gellhorn et al. (2012)

Gellhorn, A. C., Chan, L., Martin, B., & Friedly, J.  
**Management patterns in acute low back pain: the role of physical therapy.**  
*Spine*, 37(9), 775–782.  
https://doi.org/10.1097/BRS.0b013e3181d79a09  

This retrospective Medicare cohort study examined whether early PT for acute low back pain was associated with later surgery, injections, and frequent physician visits. Patients receiving PT in the acute phase had lower adjusted odds of surgery, injections, and frequent office use than those receiving PT later. On Hill’s criteria, the study again supports temporality, moderate strength, and plausibility. It also contributes some consistency because its findings align directionally with Fritz and Childs on downstream utilization. Its limitations are similar: no experiment, weak specificity, and no strong biological gradient. Importantly, the outcomes are service use rather than direct patient-centered endpoints such as sustained pain reduction or disability improvement. So this paper strengthens the claim that earlier PT may alter care pathways, while leaving the stronger claim that delay materially harms health only partially supported.

### Summary  
Early PT associated with lower rates of surgery, injections, and frequent physician visits.

### Hill’s Criteria Assessment  
- **Temporality**: Supported  
- **Strength**: Moderate  
- **Consistency**: Supported (aligns with Fritz)  
- **Plausibility**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Coherence**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Experiment**: Not satisfied  
- **Specificity**: Weak  

### Interpretation  
Reinforces utilization signal; does not establish causal improvement in patient outcomes.

## Childs et al. (2015)

Childs, J. D., Fritz, J. M., Wu, S. S., et al.  
**Implications of early and guideline adherent physical therapy for low back pain on utilization and costs.**  
*BMC Health Services Research*, 15, 150.  
https://doi.org/10.1186/s12913-015-0830-3  

This study examined low back pain care in the Military Health System and found that early referral to guideline-adherent PT was associated with lower utilization across measured outcomes and substantially lower low-back-pain-related costs.  ￼ Relative to Hill’s criteria, this paper modestly improves the causal case by integrating not just timing but also guideline adherence, which increases coherence and plausibility. It offers moderate strength for utilization and cost outcomes and some consistency with other claims-based studies. But it remains observational, so experiment is still absent, and it does not decisively resolve confounding by indication or referral selection. It is therefore stronger as evidence that certain PT practice patterns correlate with lower downstream system use than as evidence that delay itself causally worsens patient health. 

### Summary  
Early and guideline-adherent PT associated with lower utilization and lower costs.

### Hill’s Criteria Assessment  
- **Temporality**: Supported  
- **Strength**: Moderate (utilization)  
- **Consistency**: Supported  
- **Plausibility**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Coherence**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Experiment**: Not satisfied  

### Interpretation  
Stronger design than prior studies but still observational; supports system-level utilization effects.

## Fritz et al. (2015)

Fritz, J. M., Magel, J. S., McFadden, M., et al.  
**Early physical therapy vs usual care in patients with recent-onset low back pain: a randomized clinical trial.**  
*JAMA*, 314(14), 1459–1467.  
https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2015.11648  

This randomized trial is the most important paper in the set for Hill’s criteria because it directly addresses experiment. It found that early PT produced statistically significant improvement in disability at 3 months, but the improvement was modest; the advantage did not persist as a strong durable difference at 1 year.  ￼ Under Hill’s framework, this is the strongest support for causality in the bibliography because it directly manipulates timing of care. It clearly supports temporality, experiment, plausibility, and coherence. At the same time, it weakens any inflated causal claim because the strength of effect is small and long-term persistence is limited. In other words, the best experimental evidence supports a real but modest short-term effect, not a large, durable clinical benefit from earlier PT across routine recent-onset low back pain.  

### Summary  
Randomized trial showing modest short-term improvement with early PT; no meaningful long-term difference.

### Hill’s Criteria Assessment  
- **Temporality**: Supported  
- **Strength**: Weak to moderate (small effect size)  
- **Consistency**: Limited  
- **Plausibility**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Coherence**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Experiment**: Strongly supported  

### Interpretation  
Best causal evidence available; effect size small and not durable.

## Ojha et al. (2014)

Ojha, H. A., Snyder, R. S., & Davenport, T. E.  
**Direct access compared with referred physical therapy episodes of care: a systematic review.**  
*Physical Therapy*, 94(1), 14–30.  
https://doi.org/10.2522/ptj.20130096  

This systematic review compared direct-access PT with referred PT episodes of care for musculoskeletal conditions. It concluded that evidence suggests direct access may decrease costs and improve or maintain outcomes relative to referred care. Under Hill’s criteria, the paper is useful mainly for consistency, coherence, and analogy. It shows that a broader literature trends in a similar direction: fewer visits, lower cost, and similar outcomes when access is less mediated. However, because the included studies were largely nonrandomized and heterogeneous, this review does not materially strengthen experiment and only modestly strengthens strength or specificity. It is best read as a synthesis suggesting that access liberalization is not obviously harmful and may improve efficiency, while still leaving strong causal claims underdetermined.

### Summary  
Direct access PT associated with lower cost and similar or better outcomes.

### Hill’s Criteria Assessment  
- **Consistency**: Moderate  
- **Plausibility**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Coherence**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Experiment**: Weak (nonrandomized studies)  

### Interpretation  
Supports access argument, but based on lower-quality evidence.

## Sun et al. (2018)

Sun, E., Moshfegh, J., Rishel, C. A., et al.  
**Association of early physical therapy with long-term opioid use among opioid-naïve patients with musculoskeletal pain.**  
*JAMA Network Open*, 1(8), e185909.  
https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2018.5909  

This cross-sectional claims analysis examined opioid-naïve adults with new musculoskeletal pain and found that early PT was associated with reduced subsequent opioid use, including lower odds of chronic opioid use for some regions such as knee pain and low back pain.  ￼ In Hill terms, this paper supports temporality imperfectly, because exposure timing still precedes later opioid outcomes within the claims structure, and it offers moderate plausibility and coherence. The direction of effect is clinically meaningful from a systems standpoint. But experiment is absent, specificity is weak because provider choice and patient selection are deeply entangled, and consistency varies by body region. This is important evidence for downstream utilization substitution, especially away from opioids, but it is not strong evidence that earlier PT broadly prevents major deterioration in core clinical outcomes. 

### Summary  
Early PT associated with reduced long-term opioid use.

### Hill’s Criteria Assessment  
- **Temporality**: Not satisfied  
- **Strength**: Moderate  
- **Consistency**: Variable by condition  
- **Plausibility**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Experiment**: Not satisfied  

### Interpretation  
Strong utilization substitution signal; limited causal inference.

## Kazis et al. (2019)

Kazis, L. E., Ameli, O., Rothendler, J., et al.  
**Association of initial healthcare provider with opioid use for new-onset low back pain.**  
*BMJ Open*, 9(9), e028633.  
https://doi.org/10.1136/bmjopen-2018-028633  

This nationwide retrospective cohort compared opioid use outcomes based on the initial provider seen for new-onset low back pain. Initial care from conservative therapists was associated with lower odds of both early and long-term opioid use than initial care from many medical providers. Under Hill’s criteria, the paper contributes to plausibility, coherence, and some consistency with Sun. It also reinforces the idea that care pathway entry point matters. But because the exposure is initial provider type rather than randomized assignment or a cleanly isolated timing intervention, specificity is weak and residual confounding is substantial. This study therefore supports a pathway-level argument that earlier conservative entry may reduce later opioid exposure, while offering only limited causal leverage on the broader claim that delays in PT themselves produce poor health outcomes.

### Summary  
Initial provider type strongly associated with opioid exposure.

### Hill’s Criteria Assessment  
- **Temporality**: Supported  
- **Plausibility**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Coherence**: Strong but possibly tautological   
- **Experiment**: Not satisfied  
- **Specificity**: Weak  

### Interpretation  
Supports pathway effects; heavily confounded.

## Overall Synthesis

Across this literature:

- Strongest evidence:
  - **Temporality**
  - **Plausibility**
  - **Coherence**

- Moderate evidence:
  - **Consistency** (primarily observational)

- Weak evidence:
  - **Experiment** (one RCT with small effects)
  - **Strength of clinical outcomes**
  - **Specificity**

### Conclusion

The literature supports:

- Associations between early PT and:
  - reduced downstream utilization  
  - reduced opioid exposure  
  - lower episode-level costs  

The literature does **not strongly support**:

- Large, consistent, causal improvements in:
  - pain  
  - function  
  - disability  

### Implication

Evidence supports pathway-level effects but remains insufficient to justify broad system-level policy changes without further causal and economic analysis.

## Overall assessment under Hill’s criteria
Taken together, this bibliography supports a restrained conclusion. The evidence is reasonably good for the claim that earlier PT or earlier conservative-entry pathways are associated with lower downstream utilization, lower opioid exposure, and in some settings lower episode-level costs. That conclusion rests mainly on temporality, plausibility, coherence, and moderate consistency across observational studies, with one meaningful contribution from experiment through the JAMA trial. The case is much weaker for a broader causal claim that delays in PT, on the order commonly introduced by prior authorization, produce large and consistent deterioration in pain, disability, or morbidity across general outpatient populations. On Hill’s criteria, the literature is directionally suggestive but not decisive for that stronger claim.
